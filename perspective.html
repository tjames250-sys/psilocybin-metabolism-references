Curiosity

My interest in drugs began with tryptamines and magic mushrooms.

The original question was naive but sincere: What are hallucinations? Are they distortions created inside the brain, or perceptions of something that already exists beyond our ordinary awareness?

Chemistry initially seemed inaccessible. Its terminology felt like a gate built around knowledge, one more way for institutions and credentialed authorities to tell ordinary people that understanding was not meant for them.

Then came the connection between serotonin and psilocin. Both are tryptamines, and psilocin is structurally similar enough to serotonin that several serotonin receptors respond to it. That relationship opened a door. Drugs stopped looking like isolated moral objects and became part of a larger system involving plants, bodies, receptors, chemistry, culture, commerce, and law.

This was during an era when nearly every illegal drug was presented as a brain-cell-killing disease in chemical form. It was less education than obedience training: drugs were bad, users were damaged, and anyone who questioned the official story was either reckless or already lost.

I questioned it anyway.

Nature, Chemistry, and Authority

Many familiar drugs have natural origins. Opium contains morphine. Heroin, or diacetylmorphine, is produced by acetylating morphine. Ephedra contains ephedrine. Cannabis produces THC. Psilocybin mushrooms produce tryptamines. Alcohol arises through fermentation. Safrole has historically been used as a precursor in the manufacture of MDMA and MDA.

The dose makes the poison.

Natural does not automatically mean safe, just as synthetic does not automatically mean dangerous. Dose is only part of the equation: purity, frequency, combinations, individual physiology, environment, and access to reliable information all influence the outcome.

The moral boundary between a "natural drug," a "manufactured medicine," and an "illegal narcotic" is not a chemical boundary. It is political and commercial. It reflects which institutions have licenses, which corporations have permission, and which citizens can be searched, arrested, medicated, or imprisoned.

A chemical can be medicine when distributed by an approved institution and contraband when possessed by a private citizen. Governments and corporations claim the authority to decide which states of consciousness are therapeutic, which are deviant, and who may alter their own mind without permission.

That contradiction became one of the foundations of my politics.

I am a major proponent of individual liberty and the personal sovereignty of private citizens, especially their authority over their own bodies and consciousness. Freedom without bodily autonomy is not freedom.

The Body's Receptive Landscape

Human beings have endogenous signaling systems involving serotonin, dopamine, endorphins, cannabinoids, and other molecules. Some compounds found in plants and fungi resemble those internal messengers closely enough to interact with the same receptors.

Those receptors did not necessarily evolve for human drug use. Still, the compatibility is remarkable. The body possesses a receptive landscape, and nature contains molecules capable of entering it.

My first reaction was simple: that cannot be an accident. The evolutionary explanation is more complicated, but the intuition led somewhere useful. It turned intimidation into study. Once the terminology stopped looking like sacred language, chemistry became another tool for questioning authority.

Serotonin Analogues and Depression

Psilocybin is converted into psilocin. Psilocin does not become serotonin or supply material for producing it. It acts as a serotonin analogue: a different molecule that can activate several of the same receptors, particularly 5-HT2A.

That distinction matters, but so does lived experience.

Personal observation

Spaced-out doses of psilocybin appeared to interrupt my episodic depression. Sometimes a single experience was followed by roughly a month without a depressive episode. The acute effects ended within hours; the apparent benefit did not.

Others have reported similar experiences, and controlled research has begun investigating sustained antidepressant effects after limited psilocybin exposure. This does not establish that every person with depression needs psilocybin. It suggests a more interesting question: might some people occasionally benefit from a serotonin analogue rather than continuous serotonergic intervention?

The comparison with MDMA is instructive. MDMA causes substantial monoamine release while also interfering with reuptake. Many users describe an emotionally hollow aftermath. Serotonin release is not the entire explanation; sleep loss, physical exhaustion, repeated dosing, other substances, and individual physiology may also matter.

Psilocin operates differently. It temporarily activates serotonin receptors without producing the same kind of large serotonin release. Mechanism alone cannot prove that it is healthier, but a brief receptor-level intervention followed by sustained relief deserves serious investigation.

SSRIs deserve scrutiny too. They can help people, and denying that would be another form of dogma. Prescription status does not place them beyond criticism. They may be prescribed reflexively, poorly monitored, or continued through institutional inertia. Adverse effects and withdrawal can be minimized, and patients are not always given enough information to make an informed choice.

Psychedelics, Risk, and Context

My experience with LSD produced more suspicion than trust. The possibility of hallucinogen-persisting perception disorder mattered to me, as did the uncertainty of the illicit market. NBOMe compounds and other psychedelics have been sold as LSD, while numerous lysergamide analogues complicate identification further. Testing can reduce risk, but prohibition prevents the ordinary quality controls expected in legal markets.

Ayahuasca represented a different relationship with drugs. Traditionally, DMT-containing plants are combined with plants containing monoamine-oxidase inhibitors. In the practices I encountered, the preparation existed within ceremony, community, personal preparation, and integration.

Those traditions are not automatically safe or beyond criticism. Yet there was something wholesome in the insistence that a powerful experience should require preparation and responsibility. The gatekeeping was not simply you are forbidden from knowing. At its best, it was you should be prepared before entering.

People have described ayahuasca as a catalyst for confronting depression, alcohol use, other substance use, and maladaptive behavior. Whether every claim survives scientific study is a separate question. The larger lesson is already valuable: pharmacology is only part of a drug experience. Context, expectation, community, preparation, and meaning also shape what follows.

Freedom, Harm Reduction, and Recovery

My support for decriminalization became active more than a decade ago. The Decriminalize Nature movement later gave organizational form to ideas I had already been developing. That work extended through Colorado, Vancouver, Portland, California, Mexico, and the Pacific Northwest.

Freedom does not require pretending drugs are harmless. It requires replacing punishment and misinformation with honesty, responsibility, and practical safeguards.

Harm reduction means accessible drug testing, sterile supplies, clean tools, accurate safety education, and spaces where people can connect without every relationship becoming a criminal investigation or a route to a dealer. It means acknowledging that people use drugs and helping them stay alive whether or not the government approves.

Portugal provided an important political example. It did not legalize an unrestricted drug market. It removed criminal penalties for possession of limited personal quantities and handled those cases administratively, while trafficking remained criminal. The principle was straightforward: resources used to arrest and cage people could instead support treatment, housing, education, harm reduction, and human reconnection.

My political beliefs eventually collided with my own conduct. I was caught dealing narcotics and entered treatment. Treatment became a stepping stone, but accepting help did not require surrendering my judgment.

Recovery and connection

Connection and the Conditions of Recovery

Drug epidemics are often narrated as if a molecule arrived alone. The molecule matters, especially when the supply is contaminated or unexpectedly potent, but trauma, shame, instability, abandonment, and social isolation can shape why people use, how use becomes compulsive, and whether a route back feels possible. These conditions do not explain every person's substance use, but they are too central to leave outside the frame.

At the bleakest edge of an epidemic, isolation and self-poisoning can become parts of the same tragedy: a person cut off from reliable care, belonging, and reasons to remain alive while the substance becomes both anesthetic and danger.

Recovery therefore cannot be reduced to abstinence, receptor chemistry, intellectual insight, or private willpower. It can also mean rebuilding safety, trust, purpose, and connection with people who help make life inhabitable again. Understanding trauma may help name the pattern; safe relationships and embodied recovery can help loosen its hold.

Evidence context: SAMHSA describes community, supportive relationships, health, home, and purpose as major dimensions of recovery. Its trauma-informed framework emphasizes safety, trust, collaboration, empowerment, voice, and choice. These frameworks support connection as part of recovery without reducing every substance-use history to trauma or isolation. About Recovery / Trauma-Informed Approaches

Some recovery communities genuinely sustain people. Others become rigid, fear-based, and toxic. Too many people leave treatment believing they must fear relapse forever, repeatedly identify themselves through their worst period, and accept one social model as the universal standard of recovery.

Some remain in meetings because they want to. Others build stable lives outside those rooms, whether they remain abstinent or develop a different relationship with substances. Their absence from a meeting is not proof of failure.

No person's recovery story is the be-all and end-all cosmic standard. The goal should be agency, stability, connection, and a life worth inhabiting, not permanent submission to a particular institution or identity.

Questions for the Law

The law creates a different problem: what happens when scientific uncertainty is converted into criminal certainty?

Federal scheduling is built around findings concerning abuse potential, accepted medical use, and accepted safety under medical supervision. Those standards are not self-interpreting. Institutions decide what counts as accepted evidence, which forms of use qualify as medical, and how much risk society will tolerate.

If psilocybin's dependence and harm potential appear low relative to many controlled and legal substances, what evidence justifies placing it among the most restricted drugs? What evidence would be sufficient to trigger an honest reevaluation, and who gets to decide?

If researchers may administer psilocybin with institutional permission, why should personal possession automatically expose a private citizen to criminal punishment? Why does the same molecule become legitimate when controlled by a licensed institution but contraband when possessed outside it?

Does criminalization reduce drug-related harm, or does it transfer that harm into contaminated markets, incarceration, stigma, and fear of seeking help?

If the dose makes the poison, why does the law so often classify the substance without adequately considering dose, conduct, context, or actual harm?

Where no other person has been harmed, what gives the state authority over the private citizen's body and consciousness?

When scientific uncertainty remains open to investigation, why does the law answer it with cages?

Federal policy milestone · April 2026

This Is What the Fight Was For

In April 2026, a White House executive order directed federal agencies to accelerate the review of qualifying psychedelic drugs, establish a Right to Try pathway for eligible patients, support state-matched psychedelic research, expand clinical-trial participation, and prepare prompt rescheduling review after successful Phase 3 trials and FDA approval.

At the signing, psychedelic-policy advocate W. Bryan Hubbard declared, “Federal prohibition of psychedelic medicine in America is over.” As a description of current law, that reaches beyond the order: psilocybin and psilocin remain federally controlled in Schedule I, and the order did not legalize personal possession. As a declaration of political direction, however, it marked a threshold that would have been difficult to imagine when this fight began.

I spent more than ten years helping push this conversation toward liberty, evidence, recovery, and the right to investigate these compounds honestly. This was what the fight was for: not the end of every restriction, but a moment when the federal government began directing its institutions to build pathways for research, treatment, and change.

That movement is also reaching state recovery policy: Louisiana has created a clinical-research initiative that connects psychedelic-assisted therapy studies with opioid-settlement resources. Follow the Louisiana policy signal.

Research After Treatment: Psilocin, 4-HIAA, and What Comes Next

At Royal Life Centers, curiosity became sustained study. What began as an examination of tryptamine metabolism grew into months of reading articles, abstracts, books, and reference material. That work eventually produced a chart tracing psilocybin, 4-PO-DMT, through psilocin and into metabolites including 4-HIAA.

Preclinical signal

What the mouse study reported

In a mouse conditioned-place-preference study, administered 4-HIAA inhibited acquisition of methamphetamine-conditioned place preference, promoted extinction, and reduced reinstatement. The researchers also reported evidence of brain penetration and changes involving serotonin in the nucleus accumbens.

Read the 4-HIAA study

Evidence boundary

What it does not establish

A mouse conditioned-place-preference model is not human addiction or recovery. The study does not establish 4-HIAA as a human treatment, nor does it show that psilocin-derived 4-HIAA produces the same result in people.

Open mechanism

Why the question remains interesting

Other testing suggests that 4-HIAA does not bind directly to the serotonin receptors examined in the way psilocin does. If it influences conditioned behavior without directly activating those receptors, what pathway is responsible? Possibilities include indirect serotonergic regulation, another molecular target, peripheral signaling, metabolism, or a mechanism not yet identified.

The larger questions extend beyond one metabolite. Psilocin is cleared relatively quickly, yet some psychological and behavioral effects appear to persist. What sustains those changes after the active compound has left the body? How much comes from receptor activity and neuroplasticity, and how much comes from the experience, its context, and what the person does afterward?

Whole mushrooms contain more than isolated psilocybin. Do trace beta-carbolines occur at concentrations capable of meaningfully altering psilocin metabolism? Do norpsilocin, baeocystin, aeruginascin, and other alkaloids contribute to the overall effect, or does the proposed entourage effect remain an attractive but unsupported hypothesis?

These questions are directions for investigation, not conclusions. Uncertainty is not evidence of uselessness. It is the beginning of research.

The Project

The brochure emerged as a way to introduce these questions to people working in health, science, treatment, and recovery. It would not announce a cure or pretend that preliminary evidence had settled anything. It would point toward the research, show the metabolic pathways, and invite a deeper examination of assumptions that have hardened into doctrine.

The purpose is not to replace one orthodoxy with another. It is to question the drugs and the claims made for them. It is to question the research, the laws, the institutions, the treatment models, and ourselves.

I come from a family of mountain movers. Some people throw a rock into a pond and influence others through the immediate reach of the waves. My family understands another kind of change: finding where steady pressure can be applied until the landscape itself begins to shift.

I come from that tradition. This project is my application of that pressure.

I am not offering a universal cure, a replacement recovery doctrine, or permission to stop asking difficult questions. I am documenting what I experienced, what I found in the research, and where the contradictions lead.

Question the drugs. Question the claims made for them. Question the laws written around them. Question treatment, recovery, authority, and me.

Then follow the evidence wherever it goes.

This is only the beginning.
