Purpose of This Project

This site supports an educational brochure and a growing reference project about psychedelics, neuroplasticity, recovery, and behavior change. The central question brings together what psilocybin does molecularly with how psychedelic compounds, therapeutic context, learning, mood, and culture may intersect in recovery-oriented work.

The site keeps clinical evidence, preclinical signals, mechanistic plausibility, and cultural interpretation in view at the same time. That structure lets promising ideas stay explorable while preserving the differences between evidence, hypothesis, and meaning.

Educational note: You're the only one who can know your body like you do. Otherwise, psychedelics, MAOIs, ibogaine, psychiatric medications, and substance-use treatment decisions are best discussed with qualified clinicians.

Open Questions

This project follows a few live questions rather than treating the field as settled. Policy and access are part of that inquiry, because research, treatment settings, harm reduction, and public education all develop inside real legal systems.

How do decriminalization, regulated access, and clinical exemption models change what recovery research and education can safely explore?
What can be learned from Portugal's public-health approach to personal drug possession, Colorado's natural-medicine framework, and Canada's narrower clinical-trial, Special Access, and exemption pathways?
Where mushrooms or related compounds are openly traded despite restrictive laws, what does that reveal about stigma, safety, quality control, and the gap between written policy and lived practice?
Can psychedelic-assisted therapy support recovery by changing mood, cognitive flexibility, learned reward, or habit loops?
Which findings are strongest in humans, and which remain preclinical or speculative?
Can secondary mushroom alkaloids meaningfully modulate subjective effects, metabolism, or duration?
How can psychonaut philosophy and clinical evidence be presented together while keeping evidence categories clear?

Recovery-Oriented Research Thread

Recovery sits at the intersection of neuroplasticity, behavior change, craving, memory reconsolidation, mood, social context, and integration. Clinical evidence anchors the strongest claims, while animal studies and mechanistic papers help frame plausible directions for future research.

For substance-use recovery, the evidence picture is still emerging and develops alongside broader mental-health findings. Psilocybin therapy, along with other tryptamine therapies, has been reviewed in relation to major depressive disorder, obsessive-compulsive disorder, and substance use disorder, which makes mood and recovery difficult to fully separate: depression, hopelessness, craving, habit loops, and relapse risk can reinforce one another in real lives, even when the research categories are distinct.

Clinical signal

Psilocybin and behavior change

Human studies have explored psilocybin-assisted therapy for alcohol use disorder and tobacco addiction. Together, they make recovery-oriented research a serious topic with room for careful follow-up.

Mood overlap

Depression and relapse risk

Mood research matters because depression, hopelessness, craving, and relapse vulnerability can reinforce one another. This makes mood research recovery-adjacent across both addiction-focused and broader mental-health studies.

Preclinical signal

4-HIAA and conditioning

A 2025 mouse study reported that 4-HIAA blocked methamphetamine-induced conditioned place preference. As a preclinical finding, it points toward future questions about learned reward and reconditioning.

Related Psychedelic Compounds in Recovery Research

Several psychedelic or psychedelic-adjacent compounds are being studied in relation to substance use, mood, or behavior change. They overlap chemically and culturally, while also carrying distinct pharmacology, risks, and research histories. Molecular similarity offers a useful starting point for comparison alongside context, mechanism, and evidence.

Tryptamine family

Psilocin, DMT, 5-MeO-DMT

These compounds share an indole/tryptamine backbone and interact with serotonin-related systems. Psilocybin is converted to psilocin; ayahuasca combines DMT with MAO-A-inhibiting beta-carbolines.

MAOI modulators

Harmine and harmaline

These beta-carbolines sit alongside tryptamine chemistry rather than inside it, and they can change tryptamine pharmacology by inhibiting MAO-A. This is central to ayahuasca and relevant to mushroom secondary-alkaloid questions.

Indole alkaloid

Ibogaine and noribogaine

Ibogaine is structurally more complex and pharmacologically broader than classic tryptamines. Observational recovery research, especially around opioids, makes it relevant to this map, with safety considerations remaining central to any discussion.

Shared theme: These compounds belong to a wider conversation about indole chemistry, altered states, and recovery-oriented research. Each has its own pharmacology and risk profile, while the connecting thread is the investigation of plasticity, mood, craving, withdrawal, and behavior-change outcomes.
