# Project Lilith: Engagement and Outreach Roadmap

**Status:** Locked for the next project phase  
**Established:** July 22, 2026  
**Primary site:** https://tjames250-sys.github.io/psilocybin-metabolism-references/

## Purpose of This Phase

Project Lilith has moved beyond being a collection of diagrams and references. It is now an independent educational synthesis connecting psilocybin, recovery research, neuroplasticity, mushroom chemistry, policy, lived experience, and open questions.

The objective of this phase is to help the right readers discover the project, understand where to begin, respond with useful feedback, and return as the research develops.

## Locked Order of Work

1. Improve reader orientation and contribution pathways.
2. Add search and social-sharing infrastructure.
3. Connect privacy-friendly measurement.
4. Prepare reusable outreach materials.
5. Conduct direct unpaid outreach and place the first twenty brochures deliberately.
6. Review results before paying for promotion or printing another large run.

Paid promotion does not begin until the site can measure discovery, page interest, downloads, and contributions.

## Phase 1: Reader Pathways

### Start Here

Create a short reader pathway for four audiences:

- **Recovery staff:** Recovery Map -> clinical context -> recovery references
- **Researchers:** Emerging Research -> Open Questions -> bibliography and contributions
- **Advocates:** recovery policy -> Culture -> Perspective
- **Curious readers:** Core Question -> Neuroplasticity -> Open Questions

Implementation decisions:

- Create `start-here.html`.
- Place a prominent Start Here link near the homepage core question.
- Add Start Here to the universal footer.
- Do not add another item to the crowded top navigation during the first pass.
- Keep each route to approximately three links and a short explanation.

### Corrections and Contributions

Create `contribute.html` with four contribution routes:

- Report a correction.
- Recommend a missing source.
- Submit emerging research.
- Suggest or refine an open question.

Initial submissions will use prepared email links rather than a third-party form. Contributors should be asked to include the relevant page, original source or DOI, evidence category, and a concise explanation.

The page will state:

> This is an independent educational synthesis. Citation indicates that a source informed the project; it does not imply that an author, institution, or publisher endorses the synthesis.

The page will discourage submission of personal medical records or requests for individualized treatment advice.

## Phase 5: Unpaid Outreach

### Direct Outreach

- Contact three to five cited authors or relevant researchers per week.
- Personalize every message around one cited paper or finding.
- Ask for corrections, clarification, or a recommended source rather than endorsement.
- Contact treatment-center educators, addiction researchers, harm-reduction organizations, psychedelic societies, integration groups, and drug-policy organizations.
- Keep a simple outreach log with date, person or organization, page shared, response, and follow-up status.

### Organic Social Channels

- Prioritize LinkedIn for clinicians, researchers, and policy professionals.
- Participate in relevant Reddit communities before sharing project links.
- Use visual research signals for Instagram, Facebook, or short-form video.
- Share one useful finding or question per post instead of repeatedly advertising the whole site.
- Link directly to the relevant section.
- Clearly distinguish personal synthesis, scientific evidence, policy reporting, and open questions.

### Community Discovery

- Submit the project to appropriate psychedelic-community directories.
- Contact local psychedelic societies and harm-reduction groups.
- Offer a short educational walkthrough or discussion rather than a promotional pitch.
- Look for university, public-health, neuroscience, addiction-medicine, and community-education events.

## Phase 6: The First Twenty Brochures

The remaining brochures are a placement pilot, not disposable advertising.

Proposed allocation:

- 5 for treatment centers, recovery organizations, or harm-reduction programs.
- 4 for university psychology, neuroscience, public-health, or addiction-medicine settings.
- 4 for psychedelic societies, integration groups, lectures, or community events.
- 3 for independent bookstores or community-health spaces with relevant material.
- 2 for direct delivery to clinicians, educators, or researchers likely to engage.
- 2 held in reserve for unexpected high-value conversations.

Placement rules:

- Ask permission before leaving a brochure.
- Explain the project to a staff member or organizer.
- Leave one or a very small number unless more are requested.
- Do not leave materials anonymously at clinics, libraries, campuses, or recovery meetings.
- Record placement date, location type, contact, and any response.
- Review traffic and feedback before printing another large run.

## Phase 7: Paid Options

Paid promotion is considered only after the first outreach and measurement review.

Preferred paid uses:

1. A memorable custom domain.
2. A tested second brochure run.
3. A table or small sponsorship at a relevant event.
4. Sponsorship in a respected recovery, neuroscience, or psychedelic-research newsletter.
5. A focused podcast sponsorship or guest appearance.
6. Professional scientific review, editing, or accessibility review.
7. Posters or research cards for approved community spaces.
8. Travel to a conference where direct relationships are possible.

Deprioritized paid uses:

- Broad Google advertising.
- Broad Meta advertising.
- Generic paid traffic without a defined audience.
- Large print runs before the placement pilot is evaluated.
- Paid press-release distribution without a specific publication strategy.

Drug, pharmaceutical, and addiction-related advertising is frequently restricted or manually reviewed. Paid platform advertising should be treated as a later experiment, not the foundation of the outreach plan.

## Measures of Progress

Review after the first 30 days of active outreach:

- Which pages receive the most entrances and repeat interest?
- Which sources send useful visitors?
- Which brochures or conversations produce visits or responses?
- How many bibliography and brochure downloads occur?
- How many corrections, source recommendations, or substantive messages arrive?
- Which audience responds most: recovery staff, researchers, advocates, or curious readers?
- Which social topics generate thoughtful discussion rather than passive reactions?
- What wording causes confusion or overstates the evidence?

Success is not defined only by raw traffic. A correction from a source author, a useful new paper, a treatment-center conversation, or a sustained research connection may be more valuable than hundreds of shallow visits.

## Editorial and Ethical Guardrails

- Preserve the recovery-forward orientation.
- Preserve the author's personal voice, individual-liberty position, and lived-experience boundaries.
- Distinguish human, animal, mechanistic, policy, cultural, and lived evidence.
- Do not present hypotheses or active trials as settled findings.
- Do not imply that cited authors endorse the project.
- Keep educational and clinical-context language visible.
- Avoid individualized medical guidance or treatment solicitation.
- Obtain permission for physical placement and reuse of third-party media.
- Maintain corrections, source dates, and version history as the project develops.
