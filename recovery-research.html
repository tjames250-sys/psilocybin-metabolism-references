What Recovery Means Here

Recovery is used broadly on this page: substance-use recovery, mood repair, habit change, cognitive flexibility, meaning-making, social support, and integration. Psychedelic research often touches several of these areas at once, which makes a clear evidence map more useful than a single claim.

The goal is to keep different kinds of support visible together: human clinical signals, preclinical findings, mechanistic hypotheses, and lived or cultural context. Each category can contribute something useful when its role is clearly named.

Clinical context: Psychedelics, MAOIs, ibogaine, psychiatric medications, and substance-use treatment decisions are best discussed with qualified clinicians, especially when withdrawal, medication interactions, cardiac risk, bipolar/psychosis history, or trauma history may be involved.

Evidence Map

Human clinical signals

Behavior change studies

Psilocybin-assisted therapy has been studied in alcohol use disorder and tobacco addiction, with broader clinical research exploring depression, distress, and related mental-health outcomes.

Preclinical signals

4-HIAA, reward, and conditioning

A 2025 mouse study reported that 4-HIAA blocked methamphetamine-induced conditioned place preference. As a preclinical signal, it raises future questions about learned reward, serotonin pathways, craving, and reconditioning.

Contextual supports

Preparation and integration

Screening, preparation, setting, therapeutic alliance, peer support, and integration help translate meaningful experiences into everyday behavior change.

Recovery-Relevant Mechanisms

Learning window

Neuroplasticity

Psychedelic research often focuses on plasticity-related pathways that may support new learning, cognitive flexibility, and updated patterns of response.

Mood layer

Depression and hope

Mood matters in recovery because hopelessness, craving, avoidance, and relapse vulnerability can reinforce one another. Improved mood may support the work of change.

Behavior layer

Habit loops and cues

Recovery-relevant questions include cue reactivity, craving, conditioned reward, identity change, and the ability to practice new responses after insight.

Related Compounds and Recovery Questions

The compounds below belong to overlapping but distinct research conversations. Their shared value here is comparative: each helps ask a different question about altered states, neurobiology, withdrawal, craving, mood, and behavior change.

Psilocybin / psilocin

Therapy and plasticity

Human studies in alcohol use disorder, tobacco addiction, and mood-related conditions make psilocybin central to the recovery map.

Ayahuasca context

DMT plus beta-carbolines

Ayahuasca research often combines DMT, MAO-A inhibition, ceremony, community, and integration, making it relevant to both pharmacology and setting.

Ibogaine context

Withdrawal and craving

Ibogaine research is especially relevant to opioid-use questions, with safety screening and medical oversight central to responsible discussion.

Recovery Policy in Motion

Louisiana · Act 956

Opioid-settlement resources meet psychedelic research

Louisiana has established a state initiative for federally authorized psychedelic-assisted therapy studies addressing opioid-use disorder, co-occurring substance-use disorders, and treatment-resistant conditions. The law connects participating academic health centers and parishes with opioid-settlement resources for eligible patients and trial-enabling work.
